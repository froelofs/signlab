Research overview

Four pillars

SignLab was founded with the general mission to pursue new foundations for scientific inquiry in sign language research and develop practical applications for sign language users and learners, leveraging recent developments in artificial intelligence, computer vision and computer graphics. Research in the lab revolves around four pillars:

  • Methods
  • Data
  • Theory
  • Applications

A measurement revolution

We believe that sign language research (and more generally, research on visual communication, also in spoken languages) is at the dawn of a 'measurement revolution', comparable for instance to the measurement revolutions that unfolded in astronomy and biology after the invention of the telescope and the microscope, respectively, in the early 17th century. Until recently, most research on visual communication has been based on relatively course-grained measurements (video recordings). We, and a growing number of like-minded researchers in other labs, are currently developing more fine-grained measurement instruments and techniques. This, we believe, will substantially accelerate progress in the field.

Five-year outlook

Over the next five years we intend to achieve the following:

  • Methods: New methodological and technological foundations for data collection, annotation, analysis, and visualization;
  • Data: A comprehensive NGT lexicon on Global Signbank with rich information about the form, meaning, and use of each sign, as well as multiple datasets obtained through experimental elicitation;
  • Theory: A deeper theoretical understanding of the semantic, syntactic, morphological, and phonological properties of sign languages, in particular the role of non-manual features such as facial expressions and movements of the body and head, which remain understudied;
  • Applications: Educational technologies to support sign language learners, in particular parents of deaf children and interpreters in training; AI technologies to make public information and services more accessible for sign language users; and interaction technologies to enhance interpreting services, e.g., making remote interpreting services more immersive using augmented reality.
